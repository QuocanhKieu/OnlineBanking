Meiji period (1868–1912)

The emperor was restored to nominal supreme power, [163] and in 1869, the imperial family moved to Edo, which was renamed Tokyo ("eastern capital"). [164] However, the most powerful men in the government were former samurai from Chōshū and Satsuma rather than the emperor, who was fifteen in 1868. [163] These men, known as the Meiji oligarchs, oversaw the dramatic changes Japan would experience during this period. [165] The leaders of the Meiji government desired Japan to become a modern nation-state that could stand equal to the Western imperialist powers. [166] Among them were Ōkubo Toshimichi and Saigō Takamori from Satsuma, as well as Kido Takayoshi , Ito Hirobumi, and Yamagata Aritomo from Chōshū.
